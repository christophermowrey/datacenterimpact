**Houston Data Center Map**

*Product Requirements and Technical Build Specification*

Initial scope: Harris County + Fort Bend County \| Primary audience: Homebuyers and residents \| Final handoff version

| **Purpose.** Use this document as the working brief for an AI coding assistant or software developer. The first release should help a prospective homebuyer understand where data centers exist or may be built, how close they are to a property or neighborhood, and what verified information is available about possible neighborhood impacts. |

# 1. Product vision

Build a public, map-first website that tracks data centers and other potentially high-impact compute infrastructure across Harris County and Fort Bend County. The product is designed for homebuyers and residents who want a clear answer about neighborhood proximity and likely impacts. It must distinguish operational, under-construction, and announced projects; preserve source citations; and present both an overall Neighborhood Impact Score and a separate Evidence Confidence rating.

## Primary user goal

A user should be able to enter an address, neighborhood, ZIP code, or place; see nearby data centers; understand their status and proximity; review verified project details and potential neighborhood considerations; and open a dedicated page for each facility.

## Core product principles

- Map-first and easy to use on desktop and mobile.

- Evidence-based: every nontrivial project claim should identify its source and date.

- Transparent about uncertainty: announced projects, approximate locations, disputed facts, and stale records must be labeled clearly.

- Decisive but traceable: provide a clear score and interpretation while allowing users to inspect the underlying facts, assumptions, missing data, sources, and confidence.

- Designed to expand beyond Houston later without rebuilding the data model.

# 2. Geographic scope

The MVP covers Harris County and Fort Bend County, including Houston, Katy, and communities that cross or closely follow those county boundaries. Store coverage as configurable jurisdiction polygons rather than hard-coding a city-only boundary. Facilities outside the launch area may remain in a private research queue, but they should not appear on the default public map.

- Confirmed launch boundary: Harris County and Fort Bend County. Katy must be supported even where municipal or postal boundaries cross county lines.

- Create an initial research inventory of up to 100 Houston-area buildings, campuses, projects, and credible candidates. This is a candidate list, not a promise that all 100 will be published. Public records must meet the inclusion and evidence rules below.

- The application should reject or gracefully handle searches outside the supported region while explaining that expansion is planned.

Use a broad inclusion model because neighborhood impacts can arise from more than traditional colocation buildings. Separate facility class from lifecycle status: a hyperscale campus may be announced, under construction, or operational, while a smaller compute site may belong to the optional layer.

# 3. Facility inclusion and classification

|------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

## 3.1 Public facility classes

| **Facility class**               | **Definition**                                                                                                                                                                                                                | **Default map behavior**                     |
|----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Major / Hyperscale Campus        | Large cloud, AI/HPC, enterprise, or multi-building campus with material power, water, generation, land-use, or neighborhood implications. Always included when credible evidence exists.                                      | Default on                                   |
| Commercial / Colocation Facility | Purpose-built or converted commercial data center serving multiple tenants or major enterprise workloads. Included even when smaller than hyperscale facilities.                                                              | Default on                                   |
| Dedicated Enterprise Facility    | Private data center operated for one company, hospital, financial institution, energy company, government entity, or similar organization. Include when scale or site infrastructure could plausibly affect nearby residents. | Default on when material; otherwise optional |
| Additional Compute Facilities    | Smaller edge facilities, crypto mining, telecom compute sites, disaster-recovery sites, unusual high-density compute, uncertain conversions, and credible candidates that do not yet fit a primary class.                     | Default off; user-toggleable                 |
| Excluded / Not a Data Center     | Ordinary server rooms, small office IT closets, retail telecom cabinets, and records disproven during research. ## 3.2 Materiality and inclusion rules

- Automatically include known large commercial and hyperscale facilities regardless of whether all resource-use figures are public.

- Include any project with credible evidence of material electrical load, on-site generation, cooling-water demand, industrial noise, air permitting, major construction, or substantial land use.

- Do not require one rigid MW or square-foot threshold for all classes. Store measurable thresholds as configuration so the project can refine them after reviewing the first 100 candidates.

- Keep facility_class, lifecycle_status, publication_status, and evidence_confidence as separate fields. Do not overload one status field to represent all four concepts.

- A campus and its individual buildings/phases must be related records. Users should see the campus-level impact while researchers can preserve building-level specifications.

# 4. MVP user experience

## 4.1 Main map page

- Full-width interactive map centered on Greater Houston.

- Search box supporting street address, subdivision/neighborhood, city, ZIP code, and recognized places.

- Status filters: Operational, Under Construction, Announced/Planned. Facility-class filters should show Major/Hyperscale and Commercial/Colocation by default; Additional Compute Facilities is a separate default-off toggle.

- Optional filters for operator, facility class, estimated size or MW, score band, evidence confidence, cooling type, power-generation type, and last-updated date.

- A legend that clearly explains marker colors/shapes and does not rely on color alone.

- Marker clustering at lower zoom levels.

- A synchronized results list, especially on mobile and for accessibility.

- A user-location button only after explicit browser permission.

- A visible “data last updated” indicator.

## 4.2 Address and home-shopping workflow

- After an address search, place a distinct home/address marker on the map.

- Calculate straight-line distance to nearby facilities and rank them nearest first.

- Provide preset distance rings of 1, 3, and 5 miles. Optional 10- and 25-mile views may be available for regional utility/infrastructure context, but must not imply direct data-center parcel impact beyond five miles.

- Show the nearest operational, construction, and announced facility separately so an announced project is not mistaken for an operating site.

- Allow the user to copy or share a URL that preserves the selected place, filters, and map position.

- Store an address-search event as a product lead because it expresses concrete home-shopping interest. Display a clear privacy notice near search, collect only the fields defined in Section 10.1, and apply configurable retention and deletion rules.

## 4.3 Marker interaction

Use a two-step interaction, matching the requested behavior:

1.  First click or tap: open a compact facility overlay/card without leaving the map.

2.  Second click on the marker, facility name, or “View full details” action: navigate to the dedicated facility page.

The overlay should include facility name, status, operator/developer, approximate or exact location label, distance from the searched address, latest verified update, confidence level, and a link to the facility page.

## 4.4 Facility detail page

- Canonical facility name and known aliases.

- Current status, confidence level, and last verified date.

- Map with facility point or site boundary. If the exact location is uncertain, display an approximate area and label it clearly.

- Address, parcel description, municipality, county, and coordinates where publishable.

- Operator, developer, owner, tenant, land purchaser, and utility provider when known; keep these as separate fields.

- Timeline of major milestones: earliest evidence, public announcement, location disclosure, permits/filings, construction start, opening, expansions, pauses, or cancellation.

- Project characteristics when sourced: campus/site acreage, building square footage, planned phases, electrical capacity, backup generation, cooling approach, water use, tax incentives, and estimated investment.

- Neighborhood context and impact evidence, with careful separation between confirmed project-specific facts and general considerations.

- Source list with title, publisher/agency, document date, URL, access date, and a brief note explaining what each source supports.

- Correction/report-an-error action.

# 5. Neighborhood impact information

The product should organize potential impacts into understandable categories and calculate a user-facing Neighborhood Impact Score. Missing facts must not silently become zero-risk assumptions. Each category must show the measured or inferred value, its source, the scoring rule used, and an Evidence Confidence rating. When project-specific data is missing, the system may use a clearly labeled conservative proxy based on facility class, scale, cooling type, generation type, status, and distance, while reducing the confidence rating.

| **Category**                     | **What the site may show**                                                                                                                              |
|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Proximity and visibility         | Distance from searched address; distance from parcel boundary when site geometry is available; visible structures only when supported.                  |
| Noise                            | Cooling equipment, generators, substations, or construction noise; show studies, permits, complaints, or measurements when available.                   |
| Power infrastructure             | Nearby transmission lines, substations, utility upgrades, or high-load interconnection information when documented.                                     |
| Water and cooling                | Cooling method and water demand when publicly documented; distinguish estimates from reported values.                                                   |
| Air and backup generation        | Permits or documented generator capacity; do not equate permitted emissions with actual emissions.                                                      |
| Traffic and construction         | Truck routes, construction duration, road improvements, or traffic studies when available.                                                              |
| Land use and zoning              | Current zoning, special-use approvals, deed restrictions, annexation, or relevant land-use proceedings.                                                 |
| Flood and severe-weather context | Floodplain and drainage context should eventually be displayed as separate authoritative map layers, not inferred solely from the data-center location. |
| Taxes and public incentives      | Tax abatements, Chapter 313/403-style agreements where applicable, grants, infrastructure support, or other incentives.                                 |
| Community record                 | Public hearings, neighborhood comments, litigation, complaints, and company responses, presented neutrally and with dates.                              |

## 5.1 Neighborhood Impact Score

Display a Neighborhood Impact Score Range from 0-100 for the searched address and for each nearby facility. The lower bound reflects supported or conservative known facts; the upper bound includes reasonable proxies for important missing inputs. Higher values mean greater potential neighborhood impact. Always show the range, its midpoint only as a secondary visual aid, and the Evidence Confidence rating.

| **Score component**                    | **Initial weight** | **Examples of inputs**                                                                                                                                 |
|----------------------------------------|--------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Proximity and scale                    | 20                 | Distance to site boundary or point; facility class; campus acreage; building area; reported or estimated MW; number of phases.                         |
| Electricity and grid infrastructure    | 20                 | Reported/estimated load, substations, transmission upgrades, interconnection evidence, and dedicated generation.                                       |
| On-site generation and air emissions   | 15                 | Fuel type, generator/turbine count and capacity, permits, testing schedule, modeled or permitted pollutants.                                           |
| Water and cooling                      | 15                 | Cooling method, water source, reported/estimated withdrawal or consumption, wastewater, drought sensitivity.                                           |
| Noise and vibration                    | 15                 | Cooling equipment, generators, transformers, construction, setbacks, barriers, studies, complaints, or measurements.                                   |
| Construction and traffic               | 5                  | Construction duration, truck traffic, road work, workforce parking, and phase overlap.                                                                 |
| Environmental and land-use sensitivity | 5                  | Proximity to homes, schools, flood/drainage constraints, wetlands, environmental-justice indicators, zoning compatibility.                             |
| Project status and uncertainty buffer  | 5                  | Announced or under-construction projects may receive a forward-looking uncertainty adjustment; missing high-impact facts cannot lower risk by default. |

- Suggested interpretation bands apply to both ends of the range: 0-19 Minimal, 20-39 Low, 40-59 Moderate, 60-79 High, and 80-100 Very High. A result spanning bands should be labeled accordingly, for example “Moderate to High.”

- Calculate address-specific contributions from every included data center within five miles and combine them with a capped cumulative formula. Data-center parcel impacts are zero beyond five miles. Two regional exceptions are tracked separately and may extend beyond five miles: (1) utility-system effects, including electricity or water rates and resource availability; and (2) dedicated transmission, generation, water, or pipeline projects tied to data-center demand. These regional effects must not be disguised as ordinary distance-based parcel impacts.

- Use exact parcel-boundary distance when available. When only a point or approximate area is known, disclose the geometry precision and reduce Evidence Confidence.

- Version the scoring methodology. Store score_version, lower_bound, upper_bound, raw component values, known-data score, proxy assumptions, normalized component scores, weights, calculation timestamp, and inputs used so every result can be reproduced.

- Provide a “Why this range?” panel with lower- and upper-bound component breakdowns, missing information, proxy assumptions, source links, and a plain-language explanation of what could move the range up or down.

- Evidence Confidence should be High, Medium, Low, or Preliminary and must never be hidden by a confident-looking numeric score.

# 6. Data quality, evidence, and confidence

## Confidence model

- High: exact site and status supported by an official filing, permit, property record, company statement, or multiple independent authoritative sources.

- Medium: project identity and general location are well supported, but exact parcel, capacity, timeline, or status remains incomplete.

- Low: credible indication exists, but key details are unconfirmed. Low-confidence records should be excluded from the default public layer or prominently labeled.

## Source precedence

Prefer primary records: permits, planning documents, utility/regulatory filings, deeds and appraisal-district records, environmental permits, incentive agreements, company filings, and official announcements. Use reputable reporting and trade publications as secondary evidence. Social media, forums, and crowdsourced submissions may be leads but should not be treated as verification by themselves.

## Record history

- Never overwrite important historical facts without retaining the previous value and effective date.

- Store each source independently and connect it to the specific claims it supports.

- Maintain a change log for status, location, ownership, name, and material project specifications.

- Show users when a record is stale or has not been reverified within a configurable period.

## Open-source map requirement

The map UI and core mapping libraries should remain open source and avoid lock-in to proprietary map rendering APIs. MapLibre is the recommended renderer. OpenStreetMap can supply the underlying map data, but basemap tile hosting, geocoding, and routing are separate operational decisions. The code must make the tile/style URL and geocoder configurable through environment variables.

# 9. Administrative and research workflow

- Private admin login; public users do not need accounts for the MVP.

- Create, edit, review, publish, unpublish, archive, and merge facility records.

- Enter exact coordinates, draw/edit a site polygon, or mark a location as approximate.

- Add sources and associate each source with milestones, metrics, organizations, and impact claims.

- Preview a facility before publication.

- Queue records for review and require a second review for low-confidence or legally sensitive claims if staffing permits.

- Bulk import/export CSV and GeoJSON.

- Detect likely duplicate facilities by name and proximity.

- Record the editor, timestamp, and reason for material changes.

- Public correction form protected by rate limiting and spam controls.

# 10. Search, SEO, accessibility, and privacy

- Facility pages use readable URLs such as /data-centers/facility-slug.

- Generate metadata, canonical URLs, sitemap entries, and structured data where appropriate.

- All map information must also be available through a keyboard-accessible list or detail panel.

- Provide sufficient contrast, visible focus states, text labels, and non-color status indicators.

- Do not expose private research notes, unpublished documents, personal contact information, or precise sensitive locations without review.

- Use first-party analytics and documented retention. Exact address-search records are permitted as leads, but access must be restricted, encrypted, auditable, and separated from public facility data.

- Include a disclaimer that the site is informational, may contain incomplete or changing information, and is not a substitute for inspections, legal advice, environmental review, appraisal, or official due diligence.

## 10.1 Address search as a lead

An exact-address search is a high-intent product event and may be stored as a lead even when the user does not create an account. The UI must clearly disclose this near the search control and link to a privacy notice.

- Store: normalized address returned by the geocoder, latitude/longitude, search timestamp, random session ID, referring page or campaign parameters, device/browser category, search success/failure, number of nearby facilities, nearest-facility distance, score range displayed, and privacy-notice version.

- IP treatment: use the full IP transiently for rate limiting and security. For analytics/lead records, prefer a truncated address or keyed hash and record coarse region rather than retaining the full IP indefinitely.

- Do not collect a name, email, or phone number unless the user separately submits a contact form. Keep anonymous address-search leads distinct from identified contacts.

- Encrypt traffic with HTTPS and restrict database access. Exact addresses and security metadata must never appear in public APIs, logs, analytics dashboards, or error messages.

- Make retention configurable. Starting proposal: full security IP up to 7 days, anonymous address lead 12 months, aggregate non-address analytics longer. Add an admin deletion function and document how a user can request deletion.

## 10.2 Geocoding decision

Geocoding converts an address such as “123 Main Street, Katy, TX” into latitude/longitude coordinates that PostGIS can compare with data-center locations. It is separate from map rendering and map tiles.

| Option                                    | Advantages                                                                                                                     | Disadvantages                                                                                                                                                                               | MVP recommendation                                                                                                                                                |
|-------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Hosted geocoder - Amazon Location Service | Low setup effort; usage-based; easy AWS IAM/network integration; broad address database; supports stored-result billing mode.  | Each search sends the address to a third party; ongoing per-request cost; storage terms and intended-use settings must be configured correctly.                                             | Recommended for the first public AWS deployment. Use server-side calls and the storage-appropriate request mode because normalized results are retained as leads. |
| Other hosted provider                     | May offer better autocomplete, pricing, or address quality in a particular region; easy to test behind the provider interface. | Another vendor account and privacy policy; pricing/retention terms vary; possible lock-in if provider-specific response fields leak into the schema.                                        | Keep as a swappable alternative; benchmark Houston/Katy/Fort Bend accuracy before launch.                                                                         |

| Browser-only geocoding                    | Can reduce application-server work in some designs.                                                                            | Exposes credentials or requires public tokens; makes reliable lead capture, abuse controls, and consistent provider behavior harder.                                                        | Do not use for exact-address lead searches.                                                                                                                       |

## 10.3 Hosting budget and operating assumptions

The stated ceiling of approximately \$2/day (about \$60/month) is realistic for an MVP serving roughly five users per day and less than one collective hour of ordinary daily use.

- Default plan: one \$10/month 2 GB Lightsail Linux instance. Move to the \$20/month 4 GB plan if PostgreSQL imports, admin tasks, or map processing cause memory pressure. AWS lists lower-cost plans as well, but 2 GB is the safer coding target for Next.js plus PostGIS.

- Keep PostgreSQL/PostGIS on the same instance initially. A Lightsail managed database starts at \$15/month and is unnecessary at this usage level unless simplified database operations are worth the extra cost.

- Geocoding and map-tile costs should be low at five daily users, but set AWS Budgets alerts at \$20, \$40, and \$55 monthly. Add per-IP/session rate limits so bots cannot create surprise usage.

- Do not design around the assumption that the server only runs for one hour per day. A public web server is normally available 24/7; Lightsail pricing is a predictable monthly bundle, which still fits the budget.

# 11. MVP acceptance criteria

- Application runs locally through documented setup steps and Docker Compose.

- Greater Houston map loads with an open-source map renderer and configurable basemap service.

- At least three public statuses are implemented and visually distinguishable: Operational, Under Construction, and Announced/Planned.

- User can search a Houston-area address/place and see a marker plus distances to nearby facilities.

- Filters update both the map and results list.

- First marker interaction opens an overlay; a subsequent action opens the facility detail page.

- Each facility page shows status, location precision, timeline, organizations, available project facts, impacts/evidence, sources, confidence, and last verified date.

- Admin can create and update facilities, sources, milestones, and site geometry without editing code.

- Source URLs and dates are preserved and displayed.

- Automated tests cover key data validation and user flows.

- Address searches are stored as lead/search-event records under the privacy and retention rules in this specification.

- Repository includes README, environment-variable example, database migrations, seed data, backup instructions, and deployment notes.

# 15. Deferred regional utility and eminent-domain research

This work is lower priority than the map MVP, but the data model must leave room for it. In Texas, most utilities seeking a new transmission line must obtain or amend a Certificate of Convenience and Necessity (CCN) from the Public Utility Commission of Texas. The utility proposes alternative routes, provides notice, and the PUC selects or modifies a route based on statutory and evidentiary factors. After approval, the utility seeks an easement; if voluntary acquisition fails, an authorized utility may pursue condemnation and compensation proceedings. The website should eventually connect these proceedings to data-center demand only when the causal link is supported by filings or other reliable evidence.

- Research PUC Interchange dockets for CCN applications, route alternatives, maps, intervenor filings, final orders, and project-need evidence.

- Track ERCOT planning reports, interconnection/load requests where public, utility rate cases, municipal utility records, groundwater/conservation-district records, and water-supply contracts.

- Represent proposed routes as line geometries with route-segment IDs, status, docket number, notice dates, affected parcels where public, and source citations.

- Do not attribute a rate increase, water shortage, power plant, or transmission line to a particular data center based on proximity alone. Require a documented project, customer, load, contract, filing, or planning relationship.

- Show regional utility effects in a separate panel from the five-mile parcel-impact range so users can distinguish local proximity from system-wide consequences.
